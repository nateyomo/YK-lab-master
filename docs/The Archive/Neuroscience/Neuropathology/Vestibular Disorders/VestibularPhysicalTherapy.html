Physiology

Hydrops

A change in the structure of the membranous labyrinth3.

Hydrops refers to labyrinth distension3.

Generally occurs in the otolithic organs (utricle and saccule) rather than the SCCs3.

Tests

  • Mastoid Vibration Test3
  • Caloric Test3
  • Dix-Hallpike
  • CN 7 Testing
    • Face symmetry
    • Eye closing
    • House-Brackmann Facial Nerve Grading Scale
  • Weber’s Test
  • Rinne’s Test
  • Head Impulse Test (HIT)
  • Dynamic Visual Acuity (DVA)
  • HINTS Test

Symptoms

Auditory symptoms

  • Tinnitis
    • Low-pitch
    • High pitch
  • Autophony: hearing one’s own voice echo in your ear3
  • Tulio’s phenomena:
  • Sound hypersensitivity
    • Internal
    • External
  • Hearing Loss

Somatosensory symptoms

  • Aural fullness

Balance

Signs

  • Yaw plane signs
    • horizontal nystagmus4
    • past pointing4
    • rotational and lateral body falls4
    • Horizontal deviation of perceived straight-ahead4
  • Roll plane signs
    • torsional nystagmus4
    • skew deviation4
    • ocular torsion4
    • tilts of head, body, and perceived vertical4
  • Pitch plane signs
    • upbeat/downbeat nystagmus4
    • forward/backward tilts and falls4
    • vertical deviations of perceived straight-ahead.4

Unstable/Progressive Unilateral Vestibular Disorders

Meniere’s Disease

  • Hydrops (Hydropic changes) almost always3
  • Increases risk with age3
    • Almost never in children3
    • Rarely in young adults3
  • Ethnicity
    • ↓ African Americans3
    • ↑ European descent3
  • Re-occurrence
    • 1/5 patients have meniere’s disease on the opposite side later in life3
  • Otolithic Crisis Events of Tumarkin
  • Increased risk of Secondary BPPV3

Otolithic Crisis Events of Tumarkin

  • Conscious drop attacks3
  • No warning3
  • Violent3
  • Brief3
  • Generally late stages of Meniere’s disease3
  • No vertigo3
  • Feels as if you were pushed3

Vestibular Schwannoma (Acoustic Neuroma)

Growth which impinges the CNVIII Vestibulocochlear n. resulting

  • MRI
    • Internal Auditory Canal (IAC) MRI + Contrast is best3
    • Normal cuts of the brain have poor visibility of CNVIII3

Common Symptoms

  • Unilateral and insidious Hearing loss3
  • Dizziness (1/5)3
  • Unilateral insidious Tinnitus3

Treatment

  • Resection
    • Risk of CNVII Facial n. damage3

Semicircular Canal Dehiscence (SCD)

  • Superior Canal Dehiscence (SSCD)

absence in bone overlying the superior canal3

CAT scans are helpful in identifying3

Note

This can occur in other canals, but most commonly occurs involving the superior canal3.

Caution

Just because a patient appears to have SCD per imaging, does not mean we can diagnose them3.

The conclusion from imaging can only be that a patient “does not have SCD” or “may have SCD”3.

  • Pressure sensitivity
    • Sneezing
    • Coughing
    • Valsalva3
    • Lifting3
    • Bowel movements3
  • Imbalance
    • Worsened with head movement3
    • Dark environments3
  • Sound Sensitivity
    • Internal: Sound coming from your own body
      • Heel strike3
      • Eye movement3
      • Heartbeat3
      • Autophony3
    • External (sound from the environment)

Tullio’s phenomena: nystagmus induced by sound3

Note

There is increase internal sound hypersensitivity since the dehiscence causes amplification in the ear of vibrations within the body3

Testing:

  • Pressure provocation nystagmus test

    • Put pressure into the ear using a pneumatic otoscope to increase (positive) and decrease (negative) pressure into the ear
    • Sometimes the pressure can move into the superior canal in SCD patients3
    • The changes in pressure billow the cupula3
    • This results in nystagmus3

  • Sound provoked nystagmus test

  • Vent testing

Management

  • Therapy has little improvements

  • Surgical repair is effective

  • Minor symptoms

    • Education
    • Education that they are not having psychiatric symptoms

Peripheral vs Central

  • Nystagmus
    • Does not change direction with gaze for peripheral3
    • Changes direction with gaze for central disorders3
