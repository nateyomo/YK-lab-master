Anatomy

Peripheral Vestibular system

The peripheral vestibular system functions to:

  1. Provide sensory input to help stabilize visual images on the fovea of the retina2
  2. Maintain postural stability
  3. Sensory input for spatial orientation2

The semicircular canals are found within the skull between the sphenoid bone and occipital bone2.

The membranous vestibular labyrinth contains 3 semicircular canals and 2 Otolith organs structures that detect head acceleration and deceleration2.

Semicircular Canals

There are 3 Semicircular canals (SCCs) per labyrinth:

  1. Horizontal Semicircular canals
  2. Posterior/Inferior Semicircular canals
  3. Superior/anterior Semicircular canals

All of these SCCs function to detect angular acceleration2. Each SCC is oriented orthogonally (90°) relative to the next, allowing each to measure a different direction of angular acceleration.

The SCCs are filled with the endolymph fluid which is more viscous than water and freely moves with angular head accleration2.

The SCCs’ have an ampulla which is an enlargement at one end of the SCC which houses the cupula4. The cupula is attached both superiorly and inferiorly in the ampulla4.

Within the cupula is the cilia

All movements of the head result in an angular acceleration, but in different planes:

SCC Orientation

None of the canals are oriented parallel to the sagittal, transverse, or frontal planes.

Each canal on the R side is parallel with another canal on the L side which together form a functional pair4. During angular movement along the plane of the functional pair, one aspect will be excitatory+ and the other will be inhibitory-

Otolith Organs

Function

In healthy individuals, the cupula weighs the same as the endolymph surrounding it4. As a result, when laying supine, gravity does not affect the cupula’s position relative to the endolymph4.

Hair Cells

When the hair cells move towards the kinocilia, this results in excitation+. When the hair cells bend away from the kinocilia, this result in inhibition-

When the hair cells are in neutral, there is tonic activity

Step-by-Step

Angular Acceleration

  • The head experiences an angular acceleration
  • The endolymph is freely moving and remains stationary
  • The cupula is attached to the ampulla and accelerates in sync with the head.
  • The cupula accelerates with the head, but experiences resistance from the stationary endolymph
  • The endolymph resists the push by the cupula resulting in billowing of the cupula in the opposite direction of the angular acceleration
  • Billowing of the cupula displaces the cilia and associated hair cells within the cupula
  • The hair cells send an excitatory or inhibitory signal to the vestibular root of the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII)

Cupula Dysfunction

When drinking alcohol, the alcohol can be absorbed into the cupula4. Since alcohol is lighter than endolymph, the cupula now weighs less than the endolymph around it. When moving from standing to supine, the cupula weighs less than the endolymph around it and floats away from the pull of gravity. This results in vertigo or colloquially referred to as “the spins”. This pathological state is known as Positional Alcohol Nystagmus.
